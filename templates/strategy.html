Strategy & Transformation

How I think about strategy: from industrial exposure to structured business problem-solving.

🧠 My Approach to Strategy

Strategy isn't theory for me—it's grounded in understanding how real systems work. My approach combines three elements:

1. Structured Problem-Solving

Start with problem framing before jumping to solutions. Break complex issues into MECE components. Use issue trees to isolate where impact lives. Hypothesis-driven thinking over guesswork.

2. Systems-Level Thinking

Understand interconnections and leverage points. One change creates ripples—map them first. Identify where inefficiencies emerge. Think in terms of inputs → transformations → outputs.

3. Economic & Financial Reality

Ground decisions in unit economics, cost structures, profitability drivers. Understand resource allocation. Think in terms of trade-offs (growth vs. margin, scale vs. quality). Strategy without financial logic is wishful thinking.

🎯 Strategic Frameworks I Use

Structured approaches grounded in systems thinking

MECE Structuring

What it is: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive—break problems without overlap or gaps.

Applied at TKIL: Mapped sourcing flows, operational processes, cost structures. Each category clear, no blind spots.

Why it matters: Prevents analysis blind spots. Ensures every relevant factor is addressed.

Issue Trees

What it is: Decompose a big question into nested sub-questions. Isolate where the real impact is.

Applied at TKIL: "Why are supplier relationships fragile?" → "Lack of data visibility?" "Competing priorities?" "Quality inconsistency?" Each branch testable.

Why it matters: Focuses effort on 20% that drives 80% of impact. Avoids solving wrong problems.

Hypothesis-Driven Analysis

What it is: Start with directional hypothesis, test with data. Faster than exhaustive exploration.

Applied at TKIL: Hypothesis: "Procurement delays stem from supplier visibility gaps." Tested through data analysis, stakeholder interviews, process mapping.

Why it matters: Gets to root cause faster. Less wasted analysis on dead ends.

First Principles Thinking

What it is: Reduce to fundamentals. Question inherited assumptions. Build understanding from ground up.

Applied at TKIL: "Why do we source from these suppliers?" Not "because we always have" but "what makes economic sense given capacity, quality, cost, reliability?"

Why it matters: Uncovers outdated logic. Identifies where structural changes create value.

80/20 Prioritization

What it is: Identify the vital few factors driving most impact. Ruthlessly focus on them.

Applied at TKIL: 3 suppliers drove 60% of volume. 5 cost drivers accounted for 80% of spend. Focused improvement efforts there.

Why it matters: Limited resources demand ruthless prioritization. Spreads thin effort is ineffective.

Systems & Interdependencies

What it is: Understand how parts connect. Map cause-and-effect. Identify leverage points.

Applied at TKIL: Supplier quality impacts manufacturing uptime impacts customer delivery impacts brand reputation. One area of friction cascades.

Why it matters: Local optimization can hurt global performance. Must think holistically.

🔄 How TKIL Thinking Applies to Product & Business Strategy

Industrial exposure → Modern problem-solving

From: Operational Optimization

To: Product & Feature Prioritization

Industrial thinking: Focus on 20% of improvements that drive 80% of efficiency gain. Product thinking: Prioritize 20% of features that drive 80% of user value. Same rigor, different domain.

From: Supply Chain Mapping

To: Business Model & Value Chain Analysis

TKIL: Understand supplier relationships, cost flows, interdependencies. Strategy: Map competitive value chains, identify where differentiation matters, design business models accordingly.

From: Process Improvement

To: Transformation Strategy

TKIL: Identify systemic inefficiencies, structural changes that enable improvement. Strategy: Apply same rigor to business transformation, organizational change, market entry strategies.

From: Cost Structure Thinking

To: Unit Economics & Profitability

TKIL: Understand what costs money and where leverage exists. Product: Design business models, pricing strategies, and growth plans grounded in sustainable economics.

From: Cross-functional Alignment

To: Stakeholder Management & Execution

TKIL: Work across operations, procurement, supply chain. Product: Align engineering, design, business, sales around clear strategy. Same principle: clarity and shared understanding drive action.

From: Recommendation Development

To: Strategic Product Direction

TKIL: Develop options, present trade-offs, let stakeholders decide. Strategy: Same approach—structured analysis, clear options with trade-offs, defensible reasoning.
